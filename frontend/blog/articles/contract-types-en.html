Types of Construction Contracts: Comprehensive Guide

January 7, 2026 14 min read

üìú Why Does Contract Type Matter?

Choosing the right contract type is a strategic decision that affects risk distribution between owner and contractor, payment method, and change management. There's no "best" type, but rather the "most suitable" for each situation.

üíµ 1. Lump Sum / Fixed Price Contract

Description:

A total fixed price for the entire project is agreed upon before starting. The contractor commits to delivering all work at this amount regardless of actual costs.

Advantages:

  • For Owner: Knows final cost upfront (defined budget).
  • For Contractor: Opportunity for higher profit if executed efficiently.
  • Easier administration since price is fixed.

Disadvantages:

  • Requires complete and detailed drawings and specifications before contracting.
  • Contractor bears all price increase risks.
  • Changes are costly and complex.

When to Use?

When drawings are 100% complete, specifications are clear, and few changes are expected.

üìè 2. Unit Price / Re-measurement Contract

Description:

A price is set for each work unit (e.g., $100/m¬≥ concrete). Actual executed quantities are measured and payment is based on them.

Advantages:

  • High flexibility to handle quantity changes.
  • Can start before design completion.
  • Greater fairness - payment for actual quantities only.

Disadvantages:

  • Owner doesn't know final cost upfront.
  • Requires continuous effort to measure and approve quantities.
  • Disputes may arise over measurement methods.

When to Use?

When quantities are not precisely defined or expected to change (like excavation works).

üí≥ 3. Cost Plus Contract

Description:

Owner pays all actual contractor costs + a percentage (Fee) as profit and administrative expenses.

Advantages:

  • Maximum flexibility for changes.
  • Can start immediately without complete design.
  • No risk for contractor from price increases.

Disadvantages:

  • No budget ceiling (complete risk on owner).
  • Contractor has no incentive to reduce costs.
  • Requires strict financial oversight from owner.

When to Use?

In emergencies, restoration, or when scope is completely unclear.

üîÑ Hybrid Contracts

In reality, many contracts combine multiple types:

  • GMP (Guaranteed Maximum Price): Cost Plus but with a maximum ceiling.
  • Target Cost: Incentive for sharing savings or losses.
  • Lump Sum + Unit Rates: Lump sum for fixed works + unit rate for variable ones.
