Advanced EVM Techniques and Forecasting (EAC, VAC, TCPI)

January 7, 2026 14 min read

üîÆ The Art of Reading the Future

We stopped in previous articles at knowing "Where are we now". But the question the owner or investor always asks is: "How much will the project cost in the end?" and "Will we finish on time?". This is where the power of Forecasting in EVM comes in.

1. Estimate at Completion (EAC)

This number tells us: "Based on our current performance, what will the final bill for the project be?".
There are 3 main ways to calculate it, depending on your optimism or pessimism:

A. The Optimistic Method (Current Scenario Continues)

We assume that the current cost performance (CPI) will continue until the end of the project. This is the most common formula.

EAC = BAC / CPI

* BAC: Budget at Completion (Original Budget).
Example: Our budget is 100,000 and current performance CPI = 0.8 (Bad).
EAC = 100,000 / 0.8 = 125,000. (We will need an extra 25k).

B. The "Severely" Pessimistic Method

We assume we will suffer from both cost problems (CPI) and schedule problems (SPI) together for the remaining work. Used when we are bound by a strict deadline.

EAC = AC + [(BAC - EV) / (CPI √ó SPI)]

2. Estimate to Complete (ETC)

The practical question: "How much money do we need in the bank today to finish the rest of the project?"

ETC = EAC - AC

3. Variance at Completion (VAC)

Will we be winners or losers at the end of the game?

VAC = BAC - EAC

  • Positive Value (+): We will save money! üí∞
  • Negative Value (-): We will need extra budget! üí∏

4. To-Complete Performance Index (TCPI)

This is the "Pressure" indicator. It tells you the efficiency required for the remaining work to meet the original budget.

TCPI = (BAC - EV) / (BAC - AC)

If TCPI = 1.2, it means: "You must produce $1.20 worth of work for every dollar you spend from now on to recover past losses". This is often hard to achieve!

Conclusion

Forecasting is not fortune-telling; it is precise mathematics. Using these formulas gives you the power to negotiate with the owner or upper management with numbers and facts, not expectations and feelings.
