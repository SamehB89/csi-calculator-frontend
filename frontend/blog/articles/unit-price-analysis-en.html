Unit Price Analysis in Construction Projects

January 7, 2026 12 min read

üìä What is Unit Price Analysis?

Unit Price Analysis (or Rate Analysis) is the process of breaking down the price of any item in the Bill of Quantities (BOQ) into its fundamental components to accurately determine the true cost. This process is the cornerstone of professional pricing and enables contractors to submit competitive yet profitable bids.

When submitting a bid, a pricing engineer must know exactly "where the number comes from" for each item. This is fundamentally different from guessing or blindly transferring prices from previous projects.

üîß The Four Components of Unit Price

Every unit price consists of four main elements:

1. Material Cost

Includes all raw materials required to execute the item, such as cement, sand, steel, and bricks. When calculating, consider:

  • Current Prices: From approved suppliers (get recent quotations).
  • Wastage: Varies by material type (e.g., bricks 5%, tiles 10%, steel 3%).
  • Transportation: From supplier to site.
  • Storage: If there are additional storage costs.

2. Labor Cost

Depends on Productivity Rates according to CSI standards or local experience:

  • Productivity Rate: How many units does a worker/crew produce per day?
  • Crew Composition: Number of skilled, unskilled workers, and supervisors.
  • Labor Wages: Daily or hourly rate for each category.
Labor Cost per Unit = (Daily Crew Wages) √∑ (Daily Productivity)

3. Equipment Cost

Includes machinery and equipment needed to execute the item:

  • Equipment Rental: By hour, day, or month.
  • Fuel and Oil: Daily fuel consumption.
  • Operator: Operator wage if not included in rental.
  • Maintenance: Depreciation and periodic maintenance percentage.

4. Overhead & Profit

Added as a percentage on total direct costs:

  • Overhead: Office expenses, management, insurance, bonds (typically 10-15%).
  • Profit: Target profit margin (varies by competition and risk).

üí° Tips for Accurate Pricing

  • Use local productivity rates, not just international ones.
  • Get quotes from at least 3 suppliers.
  • Don't forget wastage, transportation, and storage.
  • Document every analysis for future reference.
