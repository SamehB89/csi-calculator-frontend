üéØ What is Quantity Take-off?

Quantity Take-off (QTO) or Material Take-off (MTO) is the process of extracting material quantities and work items from engineering drawings and specifications. This process is fundamental for:

  • Preparing the Bill of Quantities (BOQ)
  • Accurate project cost estimates
  • Material procurement planning
  • Progress measurement during construction

üìä Types of Take-off

1. Approximate Take-off

Accuracy: ¬±25-30%

Used in early project stages to estimate total cost:

  • Based on gross areas and estimation factors
  • Fast and doesn't require detailed drawings
  • Uses parametric data ($/m¬≤, $/bed, etc.)

Best for: Feasibility studies, conceptual budgeting, early go/no-go decisions

2. Detailed Take-off

Accuracy: ¬±5-10%

Used when submitting bids and executing projects:

  • Based on careful reading of every drawing
  • Measures each element individually
  • Takes considerable time and requires expertise

Best for: Tender submission, contract bidding, cost control baseline

üîß Detailed Take-off Process

1
Study Drawings
Understand the entire project before starting. Review architectural, structural, and MEP drawings together.
2
Identify Items
Divide work according to BOQ structure or WBS. Use standard measurement rules (SMM, NRM, POMI).
3
Measure
Use scale rulers (manual) or software (AutoCAD, Bluebeam). Record all dimensions systematically.
4
Calculate
Apply correct engineering formulas. Keep track of units (m, m¬≤, m¬≥, kg, etc.).
5
Add Wastage
Apply appropriate wastage factors for each material based on site conditions.
6
Review
Check calculations at least twice. Use different method for validation (e.g., reverse calculation).

üí° Professional Tips for Accurate Take-off

üìã Organization Tips

  • Use consistent units throughout all calculations
  • Document the source of every number (sheet, detail)
  • Create standard templates for repetitive work
  • Use color coding for different trades

‚úÖ Verification Techniques

  • Check take-off using different method (reverse calc)
  • Compare with historical data from similar projects
  • Have independent person review critical items
  • Use ratio checks (kg rebar/m¬≥ concrete)

‚ö†Ô∏è Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Forgetting hidden elements (foundations, MEP)
  • Confusing net and gross areas
  • Ignoring specification requirements
  • Not reading all addenda and revisions
  • Using wrong scale on drawings
