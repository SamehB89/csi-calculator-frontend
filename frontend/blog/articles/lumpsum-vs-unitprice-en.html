Lump Sum vs Unit Price Contracts: Detailed Comparison

January 8, 2026 12 min read

⚖️ Introduction: Why This Comparison Matters

Choosing between Lump Sum and Unit Price contracts is a critical decision affecting risk allocation, payment method, and change management throughout the project lifecycle. Understanding their fundamental differences helps owners and contractors make the right choice.

📋 Lump Sum Contract (Fixed Price)

Definition:

A contract that commits the contractor to execute all project works for a fixed, predetermined total amount. This amount only changes through formal change orders.

How It Works:

  • Contractor submits an all-inclusive total price for all works.
  • Payment is made in installments based on completion percentage or milestones.
  • Any cost overrun is borne by the contractor.
  • Any savings belong to the contractor.

When to Use:

✅ When drawings and specifications are 100% complete
✅ Few scope changes are expected
✅ Owner wants to know final cost upfront
✅ Traditional building projects with complete design

📏 Unit Price Contract (Re-measurement)

Definition:

A contract where a price is set for each work unit (e.g., $/m³ concrete or $/LM pipes). Actual executed quantities are measured and payment is based on them.

How It Works:

  • Contractor submits a schedule of unit rates for each item.
  • Actual executed quantities are measured periodically.
  • Payment = Actual Quantity × Unit Rate.
  • Final cost is determined after work completion.

When to Use:

✅ When quantities are not precisely defined
✅ Excavation and infrastructure projects
✅ Owner accepts flexibility in final cost
✅ Maintenance and repair projects

💰 Practical Example

Scenario:

Residential building construction, estimated quantities: 1000 m³ concrete

Under Lump Sum Contract:

  • Contractor submits total price: $500,000
  • If actually executes 1100 m³ → bears the difference
  • If actually executes 900 m³ → keeps the savings

Under Unit Price Contract:

  • Contractor submits rate of $500/m³
  • If actually executes 1100 m³ → receives $550,000
  • If actually executes 900 m³ → receives $450,000

⚠️ Key Considerations

  • In Lump Sum: Study drawings carefully and add risk contingency.
  • In Unit Price: Clearly define Method of Measurement in contract.
  • For Owners: Beware of "Unbalanced Bidding" in unit price contracts.

🔄 Hybrid Contracts

In practice, both types can be combined:

  • Lump Sum + Unit Rates for Changes: Base works at fixed price, changes at agreed unit rates.
  • Unit Price with Cap: Protects owner from budget overrun.
